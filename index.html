The 5-Layer Resume Analysis Method

1 First Impression Scan (10 seconds)

Read the resume as a recruiter would. Note:

  • Visual clarity and formatting consistency
  • Whether key qualifications jump out immediately
  • Overall length (ideal: 1-2 pages for most roles)
  • Use of white space and readability
üí° Coach's Script: "If I only had 10 seconds to review this, what would I learn about you? Let's make sure your strongest assets are immediately visible."
2 Header & Contact Information

Verify essentials are present and professional:

  • Full name (larger font, clearly visible)
  • Phone number and professional email (no [email protected])
  • LinkedIn URL (customized, not default number)
  • Location (city, state - no full address needed)
  • Portfolio/website links if relevant
Common Fix: Replace objective statements with a 2-3 line professional summary highlighting years of experience, key expertise, and unique value proposition.
3 Achievement-Based Experience Review

Transform task lists into accomplishment statements:

‚ùå Weak (Task-Focused) ‚úÖ Strong (Achievement-Focused)
Managed social media accounts Grew Instagram following by 347% (5K to 22K) in 6 months, generating 15% increase in web traffic
Responsible for customer service Maintained 98% customer satisfaction rating while resolving 50+ daily inquiries, reducing response time by 40%
Led team meetings Facilitated weekly cross-functional meetings with 12+ stakeholders, improving project delivery time by 25%
üí° Formula to teach clients: Action Verb + Task + Quantifiable Result + Context/Impact
Example: "Implemented new CRM system, reducing data entry time by 30% and improving lead conversion rate from 12% to 18%"
4 Keywords & ATS Optimization

Ensure the resume passes Applicant Tracking Systems:

  • Extract keywords from target job descriptions
  • Integrate industry-specific terminology naturally
  • Use standard section headers (Experience, Education, Skills)
  • Avoid tables, text boxes, headers/footers (ATS can't read these)
  • Save as .docx or PDF (check which format client prefers)
  • Include both acronyms and full terms (SEO and Search Engine Optimization)
2026 Trend: AI-powered ATS systems now scan for skills mentioned in context, not just keyword stuffing. Ensure skills appear in achievement statements, not just a skills list.
5 Final Polish & Consistency Check

Professional details matter:

  • Consistent date formatting (Jan 2024 or 01/2024, not mixed)
  • Parallel structure in bullet points (all start with action verbs)
  • Consistent tense (current job = present tense, past jobs = past tense)
  • No typos or grammatical errors (use Grammarly or similar)
  • Consistent font (one for headers, one for body - max 2 fonts)
  • Appropriate margins (0.5"-1" all sides)

Before & After Example

Before: "Worked on marketing campaigns and helped the team with various projects. Managed social media."

After: "Designed and executed 12+ integrated marketing campaigns generating $2.3M in revenue. Managed 4-person team and grew social media engagement by 215% across LinkedIn, Instagram, and Twitter platforms."

Industry-Specific Interview Preparation

Tech/Engineering Interviews

Key Focus Areas: Technical skills, problem-solving, system design, cultural fit

Sample Questions & Coaching Points:

  • "Walk me through your approach to debugging a production issue."
    Coach on: STAR method, emphasizing methodology, collaboration, and learning from incidents
  • "How do you stay current with emerging technologies?"
    Coach on: Specific examples (courses, projects, conferences), continuous learning mindset
  • "Describe a time you had to make a trade-off between perfect code and shipping on time."
    Coach on: Business acumen, pragmatism, technical debt management
üí° 2026 Tech Trend: Prepare for AI collaboration questions: "How have you integrated AI tools into your workflow?" or "How would you validate AI-generated code?"

Leadership/Management Roles

Key Focus Areas: Team building, conflict resolution, strategic thinking, results delivery

Sample Questions & Coaching Points:

  • "Tell me about a time you had to give difficult feedback to an underperforming team member."
    Coach on: Empathy + accountability, specific examples, positive outcomes or lessons learned
  • "How do you prioritize when everything is urgent?"
    Coach on: Framework (Eisenhower Matrix, ROI analysis), stakeholder communication, delegation
  • "Describe your leadership style and how you adapt it to different team members."
    Coach on: Self-awareness, flexibility, specific examples of adaptation
Practice Exercise: Role-play a scenario where client must advocate for team resources while managing executive expectations. Coach on balancing data (metrics) with narrative (team impact).

Sales & Business Development

Key Focus Areas: Deal closing ability, relationship building, resilience, strategic prospecting

Sample Questions & Coaching Points:

  • "Walk me through your biggest sale from prospecting to close."
    Coach on: Full sales cycle demonstration, obstacles overcome, relationship elements, value articulation
  • "How do you handle rejection?"
    Coach on: Specific coping strategies, learning mindset, persistence examples, metrics (call volume after rejection)
  • "What's your approach to understanding a prospect's pain points?"
    Coach on: Discovery questions, active listening, research process, consultative selling

The STAR Method Mastery

Teach clients to structure answers effectively:

Component What to Include Time Allocation
Situation Context, challenge, or problem. Be specific but concise. 15-20%
Task Your responsibility, goal, or what was at stake. 10-15%
Action Specific steps YOU took (not "we" - focus on your role). Most detailed part. 40-50%
Result Quantifiable outcomes, lessons learned, positive impact. 20-25%

Reverse Interview Questions Clients Should Ask

Coach clients to ask insightful questions that demonstrate engagement:

  • "What does success look like in this role after 6 months and 1 year?"
  • "What are the biggest challenges facing the team/department right now?"
  • "How does this role contribute to the company's strategic objectives?"
  • "Can you describe the team dynamics and collaboration style?"
  • "What opportunities exist for professional development and growth?"
  • "How is performance measured and feedback provided?"
  • "What do top performers in this role have in common?"
üí° Red Flag Questions to Avoid: Don't ask about salary/benefits in first interview, work-life balance (sounds like you're not committed), or questions easily answered by basic company research.

The 7-Phase Negotiation Process

1 Research & Anchor Establishment

Gather data before discussing numbers:

  • Use Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, Payscale, LinkedIn Salary for industry benchmarks
  • Factor in: location, experience level, company size, industry
  • Establish 3 numbers: minimum acceptable, target, aspirational
  • Research total compensation: base, bonus, equity, benefits, perks
üí° 2026 Research Tip: Use AI tools to analyze salary trends. Prompt: "What's the salary range for [role] in [location] at [company size] with [X] years experience in 2026?"
2 Delay Salary Discussions

Avoid naming numbers first. If asked about expectations early:

  • "I'd love to learn more about the role and responsibilities before discussing compensation."
  • "I'm looking for fair market value for my skills and experience. What range has been budgeted for this position?"
  • "My expectations are flexible depending on the full compensation package and growth opportunities."
If pressed hard: Give a range based on research, with your target as the low end. "Based on my research, roles like this typically range from $X to $Y, depending on the full package."
3 Receive the Offer (Don't Accept Immediately)

Script for receiving verbal offer:

"Thank you so much! I'm really excited about this opportunity and the team. I'd like to review the written offer carefully and discuss it with my family. When can I expect the formal offer letter, and what timeline are you working with for a response?"

Key Points:

  • Express enthusiasm and gratitude
  • Request 2-5 days to review (standard practice)
  • Get everything in writing before negotiating
  • Never accept or decline on the spot
4 Evaluate Total Compensation

Analyze beyond base salary:

Component What to Evaluate
Base Salary Compare to market rate, your minimum, cost of living
Bonus/Commission Guaranteed vs. performance-based? Realistic targets?
Equity Vesting schedule, strike price, company stage, dilution
Benefits Health insurance quality, 401k match, PTO, parental leave
Perks Remote flexibility, learning budget, wellness stipend, equipment
Growth Promotion timeline, skill development, industry exposure
5 Craft Your Counter-Offer

Professional negotiation email template:

Subject: Re: [Company Name] Offer - [Your Name]

Dear [Hiring Manager],

Thank you again for the offer to join [Company] as [Role]. I'm genuinely excited about the opportunity to [specific impact/contribution].

I've carefully reviewed the offer, and I'm hoping we can align on compensation that reflects both the market rate for this role and the value I'll bring to the team. Based on my [X years experience], [specific expertise], and [relevant achievements], I was expecting something closer to [specific number] in base salary.

I'm also hoping we can discuss [additional component: signing bonus, additional PTO, earlier review, equity, etc.]. This would make the overall package highly competitive and allow me to hit the ground running.

I'm confident we can find a solution that works for both sides. I'm available to discuss this by phone at your convenience.

Thank you for considering this request.

Best regards,
[Your Name]

6 Handle Objections & Alternative Negotiations

If base salary is firm, negotiate these elements:

  • Signing Bonus: One-time payment to bridge gap ($5K-$25K typical)
  • Performance Review Timeline: 6-month review instead of annual
  • Additional PTO: Extra 3-5 days vacation
  • Remote Work Flexibility: More WFH days per week
  • Professional Development: Conference budget, certification funding
  • Title Adjustment: Senior vs. Mid-level (impacts future earnings)
  • Relocation Assistance: If applicable
  • Start Date: Negotiate later start for current project completion
üí° If They Say "This is Our Final Offer": "I appreciate your transparency. Before I make my decision, could we explore [specific alternative like signing bonus or PTO]? This would make it much easier for me to accept with enthusiasm."
7 Close with Gratitude & Professionalism

Whether you accept, decline, or continue negotiating, maintain relationship:

  • Thank them for their flexibility and consideration
  • Reiterate excitement about the role (if accepting)
  • Get final agreement in writing before resigning current job
  • If declining: keep door open, provide honest feedback, stay connected

Common Negotiation Mistakes to Avoid

  • ‚ùå Accepting the first offer without discussion
  • ‚ùå Making it about personal needs ("I need X because of my mortgage")
  • ‚ùå Giving ultimatums or being aggressive
  • ‚ùå Negotiating multiple times after "final" agreement
  • ‚ùå Lying about competing offers (they may call your bluff)
  • ‚ùå Focusing only on salary, ignoring total package
  • ‚ùå Resigning current job before written offer signed

  2. Scenario: Company says "budget is fixed"

Navigating Challenging Coaching Situations

Even experienced coaches encounter resistance, unrealistic expectations, or difficult dynamics. Here's how to handle common challenges professionally.

Scenario 1: The Resistant Client

Symptoms: Doesn't complete homework, makes excuses, doesn't implement advice, skeptical of suggestions

Root Causes to Explore:

  • Fear of change or failure
  • Overwhelmed by action steps
  • Not truly committed (someone else pushed them to get coaching)
  • Previous bad experience with coaching
  • Mismatch between coach approach and client learning style

Coaching Response Strategy:

1 Name the Pattern Gently:

"I've noticed that the last few sessions, we've set action items but they haven't gotten done. I'm curious about what's happening. Can we talk about that?"

2 Explore the Resistance:
  • "What's making it hard to complete these tasks?"
  • "On a scale of 1-10, how committed are you to this goal?"
  • "Is this the right goal for you right now, or should we pivot?"
  • "What would make these action steps feel more doable?"
3 Adjust Approach:
  • Break tasks into smaller, less intimidating steps
  • Reduce number of action items (1-2 max instead of 5)
  • Create accountability check-ins between sessions
  • Shift from "homework" framing to "experiments" or "practice"
  • Address mindset blocks before pushing forward
4 Set Boundaries if Needed:

"I want to support you, but coaching only works if you're actively engaged. If this isn't the right time or approach, that's totally okay - we can pause or adjust. What feels right to you?"

üí° Remember: You can't want their success more than they do. Sometimes the most helpful thing is to pause the engagement or respectfully part ways.

Scenario 2: Unrealistic Expectations

Symptoms: Wants VP role with 2 years experience, expects $200K salary with no track record, wants results in 2 weeks, believes resume alone will land job

Coaching Response:

1 Validate Their Ambition:

"I love your ambition and I want to help you reach those goals. Let's talk about the most realistic path to get there."

2 Provide Market Reality (With Data):
  • Share salary data from Glassdoor, Levels.fyi for their experience level
  • Show typical career progression timelines in their industry
  • Explain hiring manager perspective on qualification gaps
  • Present job search timeline statistics (average 3-6 months)

"Based on industry data, professionals with your level of experience typically earn between $X-$Y. To reach $Z, you'd likely need [additional qualifications]. Let's create a strategic plan to get you there."

3 Reframe as Milestone Journey:
  • Short-term (3-6 months): Achieve [realistic intermediate goal]
  • Medium-term (1-2 years): Build to [next level]
  • Long-term (3-5 years): Reach [ultimate goal]
4 Get Buy-In on Adjusted Goals:

"I hear that you ultimately want [big goal]. To get there, we need to build [specific experience/skills] first. Are you willing to focus on [realistic next step] as our initial target?"

üí° Balance: Don't crush dreams, but don't enable delusion either. Your job is to help them achieve ambitious goals through realistic strategies.

Scenario 3: The Overly Dependent Client

Symptoms: Emails constantly between sessions, won't make decisions without coach approval, wants you to write their resume/emails for them, panics without immediate responses

Coaching Response:

1 Set Clear Communication Boundaries:

"I want to support you effectively, so let's clarify how we'll communicate between sessions. I check email [frequency] and respond within [timeframe]. For urgent questions, [alternative arrangement]. Does that work for you?"

2 Build Their Decision-Making Confidence:

When they seek approval, redirect:

  • "What do you think you should do?"
  • "What are the pros and cons as you see them?"
  • "If I weren't available, what would your instinct be?"
  • "There's no single 'right' answer here - trust your judgment."
3 Teach, Don't Do For Them:
  • Show them how to evaluate options, don't just give answer
  • Provide frameworks and templates, but they implement
  • Review their drafts, don't write from scratch
  • Guide their research process, don't do research for them

"I could write this for you, but you wouldn't learn the skill. Let's do it together - you draft based on the framework I gave you, then I'll provide feedback."

4 Reinforce Their Capability:

"You already know the answer - you just need to trust yourself. You're more capable than you give yourself credit for."

Scenario 4: The Scope Creep Client

Symptoms: Career coaching becomes relationship counseling, requests help with personal issues beyond your expertise, sessions run over time, wants "just one more quick thing" constantly

Coaching Response:

1 Define Boundaries Proactively:

In first session and contract, clearly state:

  • Scope of coaching (career-focused, not therapy)
  • Session duration (60 minutes means 60 minutes)
  • Topics outside your expertise
  • Referral process for specialized needs
2 Redirect Gently When Issues Arise:

"I'm hearing that [relationship/mental health/financial issue] is really affecting you. While that certainly impacts your career, it's outside my area of expertise.   • Set timer visible to both of you
  • Give 10-minute warning before session ends
  • Close firmly: "We're at time. Let's pick this up next session."

Scenario 5: The Blamer

Symptoms: Nothing is ever their fault, blames market/boss/economy, won't take responsibility, constant victim mentality

Coaching Response:

1 Validate, Then Redirect to Agency:

"I hear that the market is tough right now, and that's a real factor. And... within that reality, what CAN you control? Where do you have influence?"

2 Use the "What Else?" Technique:

When they blame external factors:

  • "That's one factor. What else might be contributing?"
  • "If that's true, what can you do differently?"
  • "What role did you play in this situation?"
3 Focus on Future, Not Past:

"I understand that experience was frustrating. Looking forward, what do you want to do differently? What can you learn from this?"

4 Challenge Gently but Directly:

"I'm noticing a pattern where external factors are always the reason things don't work out. I wonder if that perspective is serving you well, or if it might be holding you back from taking action. What do you think?"

When to Fire a Client

Sometimes the best decision is to end the relationship. Consider terminating when:

  • Client repeatedly violates boundaries (harassment, late payments, no-shows)
  • You've had honest conversation about issues but nothing changes
  • Client needs support beyond your scope (therapy, crisis intervention)
  • Personality clash prevents effective coaching
  • Client isn't taking any action despite multiple attempts to engage
  • You feel dread before sessions (trust your gut)

How to End Professionally:

"After reflecting on our work together, I don't think I'm the best fit to support you moving forward. I want you to get the most value possible, and I believe [specific type of support/different coach] would serve you better. I'm happy to provide referrals and wish you all the best."

üí° Protect Your Practice: Difficult clients can drain your energy and impact other clients. It's okay to be selective and maintain boundaries. Not every client is your client.
